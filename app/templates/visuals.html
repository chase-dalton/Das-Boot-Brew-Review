Using AI to Brew Better Beer








Balancing the art of brewing beer with modern approaches to decision-making can lead to a beautiful marriage between taste and efficiency. Das Boot's Data Analysis team has analyzed over one million beers and discovered the following relationships which are important for any successful brewery to understand.




Brewers and beer enthusiasts alike often rate beer on four categories: appearance, aroma, color and taste. Das Boot has determined that the rating metric that contributes most weightily to a beer's overall rating is the taste. Since a great tasting beer is equivalant to a great overall beer, it is useful to investigate what factors contribute to a great tasting beer.


While it may be surprising at first to learn that there is a relatively strong positive correlation between the appearance and taste of a beer, it makes sense. The type of glass that the beer is served in, the head and the head retention, and the lacing after taking a nice long sip all play an important role when it comes to enjoying a pint.


How many people want to drink a beer that smells like the men's locker room? None. There is a very strong positive correlation between aroma and taste of beer. The sense of smell is very closely linked with taste and that is as true for beer as any other food. Even a great tasting beer will not be successful if the aroma is off-putting.


There is very little correlation between color and taste of a beer. This visualization, however, includes over one million different styles of beer. A successful brewer should remember that color may not be important to overall taste, but consumers will generally expect Pilsners to be pale, and stouts to be dark. Green beer, for example, would ordinarily be unappealing, but once a year it's a fan favorite!


Alcohol by Volume is another parameter that seems to have very little effect on beer taste. There are great tasting beers of various alcohol levels across almost all beer styles.


Novice beer reviewers may question what exactly a 'palate' review might entail. Generally, this is where carbonation, mouthfeel and bitterness may contribute to a reviewer's rating. Not surprisingly then, palate is closely correlated to taste.
